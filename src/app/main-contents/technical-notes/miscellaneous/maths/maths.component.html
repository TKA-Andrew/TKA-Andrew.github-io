3-sigma Limits

  • Sigma (σ) is a statistical term representing the standard deviation of a dataset. It measures the dispersion or spread of the data points around the mean.
  • One sigma = one standard deviation away from the mean in a normal distribution
  • Commonly used sigma values include:
    • 1-sigma (σ): Represents a range covering approximately 68.27% of the data around the mean in a normal distribution.
    • 2-sigma (2σ): Represents a wider range covering approximately 95.45% of the data around the mean.
    • 3-sigma (3σ): Represents an even wider range covering approximately 99.73% of the data around the mean.
  • In many fields, such as engineering and quality control, sigma values are used to assess process performance and variability.
  • For example, let's consider an example where we have collected stopping accuracy data for a mobile robot. We'll assume that the data is measured in millimeters (mm), and we want to determine the 3-sigma accuracy and 3-sigma precision of the robot.
    • List of data: 42, -58, 45, 32, -20, -43, 24, 53, 22, 4
    • Mean (μ) = (42 - 58 + 45 + 32 - 20 - 43 + 24 + 53 + 22 + 4) / 10 = 10.1 mm
    • Standard Deviation (σ) = √[Σ(xi - μ)² / (n - 1)] ≈ 41.46 mm
    • Upper Limit = Mean + (3 * Standard Deviation) ≈ 134.48 mm
    • Lower Limit = Mean - (3 * Standard Deviation) ≈ -114.28 mm
    • 3-sigma accuracy: ±134.48mm
    • 3-sigma precision: ±(3 * Standard Deviation) = ±124.38mm
