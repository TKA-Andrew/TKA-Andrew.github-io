Basics

  • When the voltage is high enough, without even touching the wire, the electricity can reach out and touch us if we are close enough to it.
  • It's not just voltage that's dangerous, but current as well.
  • Car batteries are mostly 12V, but they can pull out hundres of Amps. The high current flow can even melt metals. Hence, huge wires are used to handle large amount of current flow without actually melting.
  • Before grabbing a wire, touch it with the back of your hand first. This is because when we get electric shocked, the muscles in our hands will tense up.
  • V = IR
  • P = IV
  • Using multimeters:
    • Voltage is measured across batteries or components.
    • Resistance is measured across a part with NO power applied to the circuit.
    • To measure the current flow, you need to break the circuit and let the current flow through the multimeter.
    • Always start measuring from highest scale.
  • Capacitors
    • Capacitors can store charges, but it is not usually what it is used for.
    • Example of reading capacitor's capacitance: 104 = 10 0000 pF
    • When capacitors are connected to a DC source, initially the current flow is high, but slowly when the capacitors store charges, its voltage increases and hence the current flow will drop to zero.
    • When capacitors are used together with an AC source, the current flow peaks again when the polarity is revesed. Hence, the higher the alternating frequency, the better the current remained .
  • Diodes
    • Diodes are used to ensure current flow in a single direction.
    • The forward bias voltage of the diodes are normally small (less than 1V). Once the forward bias is achieved, the resistance across the diodes basically drops to zero, and hence normally we need to protect the diodes with a resistor, so that less current flow through the circuit.
